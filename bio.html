All three members are working on recording and production of their 2nd album, “Awakening”, a tribute album. Expect some music videos in the near future, too.

  • Follow @MaryNorell
  • Follow @tschubring

TBF is proud to be endorsed by: Greg Deocampo, Atmosphere, Jack Ramos, Jay Gabler, Kim Dotcom, Emre Erturk, Kick Kennedy, and Tom Green.

Q: The Band Famous is currently unsigned, do you have any record labels that you see yourselves affiliated with in the near future?

Norell: I would definitely have consideration to signing to a label, or more than one. I’ve always loved Ellen, so her record label has always sort of been on the back of my mind in previous years even before beginning the TBF project.

Zander: Well, there are a couple of record labels that have already been following me over the past couple years, such as Ghostly International and Stones Throw Records. I would consider signing with them since they’ve already been in our atmosphere, so to speak. There’s even a little history of when I opened and ran an art gallery in downtown Minneapolis some years ago, that I had Solvent as one of the featured musical acts in that space, and he is represented by Ghostly.

Terry: I’m a startup developer so my first target is mobile devices. The iTunes App Store and Google Play are the natural solution in 2015 but I see a lot of opportunity on consoles and Nintendo DS.

Q: Are you currently focused more on art or music?

Zander: Actually, we have music videos planned, so in a way, both.

Norell: It is hard to weigh whether one holds more power as far as prioritizing goes because they are so intertwined. I can’t really see one without the other – you take one of them away and it’s like an empty mattress whereas the other one has full on lovemaking. So I’d say both.

Terry: I sold all of my art rock records several years ago. I’m still in an ambient phase but I’ve been covering some piano songs just to keep learning. My visual work is all done in code such as CSS and canvas so minimalism is required. For me the next level of art is interaction design where the work itself is imperceptible.

Q: Your first EP was improv. How was that recording process?

Terry: The studio powertool is the mixing board. That’s where 24 channels are committed to 8 busses, or tracks. Typically track 1 is the Simmons brain in mono, on 2 the Moog monosynth is the bass. Keyboards are submixed to stereo each with it’s own effects printed to tape. The lead vocal gets it’s own dry track and the harmonizer takes two more tracks. The last track will be a miked instrument or noise generator. Mixdown is done on a smaller board. I need meters and faders and knobs. The computer is only used for editing the master. This recording method lets the moment of inspiration be fully produced as it happens. Editing overdubs in order to create perfection is just more clicking than I can take. The mouse is not a musical instrument!

Norell: It was amazing. By creating improv material, you really go in with little expectations, except that you are about to have a very creative, expressionistic experience. When the outcome of that can be something that you actually enjoy listening to after – that is a great reward in and of itself.

Zander: A lot of times I spent time listening, other times I hummed what I would want to hear with what I was hearing in that moment, and then making a decision to feel that out on an organ, a guitar, or whatever instrument happened to be close by.

Q: How would you describe the sound of your upcoming 2nd album, Awakening? Some have been speculating about whether you will keep to your improv roots or if you will expand in another direction.

Zander: So much of both at this very moment, meaning no we will not lose our improv roots, but at the same time I believe we are expanding. Often when I record I want it to be perfect, but when I perform, I could care less. Not in the sense that I don’t care how it sounds, but that I want to let the music control me and not my lyrical and musical theoretical structure. Expect a diverse sound but the clue is all in the title. And that is something really exciting, and we are definitely looking forward to performing improv on stage. I’m not sure other musicians would want us to open for them, unless they had a chance to meet with us first.

Norell: We definitely still approach a good deal of the musical creativity in an improvised fashion. That is not to say that if we record something once we might not actually try and reform it into something totally different, to see what different shapes it can take on in an inspired form. I still have my arsenal of lyrics that is ever growing, and the lyrics will always adapt to the sounds that my band and I are producing in that moment. Some lyrics fit with improvised sound, and others require a more conceptualized and planned for execution. So yea, I’d say that listeners could expect a pretty diverse sound, with hints of our roots, as well as a taste of what our future might be. Oh and not to mention that we will be featuring a new vocalist on some tracks as well, the lovely Gisselle – she’s swell as hell!

Terry: I can’t wait to hear it!
